Notes on the Data¶

As we view the data, we note a few things:

- It is time sensitive, we have timestamps of listening.
- Each play of a song is not necessarily the whole song. (See Play that Funky Music by Wild Cherry, second row). 
- The data starts in September 2023 and ends in August 2024.
- There are no null values but there are songs with 0 playtime (presumably immediately skipped on a playlist).

Unfinished Songs¶

While we have each song's playtime, we do not have the max playtime for a song.

For example, we listened to Play that Funky Music for 9.2s, but we do not know if that is the whole song. It is obvious for this case but we do not know when it is less obvious.

We could group the songs by trackName and find the max msPlayed. This is not fullproof as if we have only played Play that Funky Music once, then the max msPlayed is 9200. Furthermore, this may filter out plays where we skip in the final few seconds.

A publication by Paul Lamere, https://musicmachinery.com/2014/05/02/the-skip/, shows that songs on Spotify have a 35.05% likelihood of being skipped in the first 30 seconds and 48.60% of being skipped before the song ends.

From this, we can conclude that 72.12% of skipped songs are skipped in the first 30 seconds. Let's count all songs less than 30 seconds and see how many we have in our dataset.

We see that 37.48% of our plays are less than 30 seconds, which closely corresponds to the 35.05% likelihood of being skipped. From this conclusion, we can make the assumption that our plays of less than 30 seconds are skips.

Careful Grouping¶

The dataframe is grouped to obtain plays per month.

Consequently, we find the idxmax number of plays within each month (the index of the song with the most number of plays per month).

These indicies (one for each month) are used to index the series and we have our most played songs of each month.

Notable Change¶

Importantly, each grouping does not sort the values. The dataset starts in September 2023 and ends in August 2024. By sorting, and placing January at the top of the series, we imply that the listening starts at January.

The order of listening data tells a story, so it is important to recognise that as you process the data.

An obvious indicator of continued listening is that month 9 and month 10 have the same top song - clearly enjoying that song over an extended period.

Conclusion¶

Overall, we answered the information that we aimed to find.

We uncovered my top 20 most played songs, the most played songs amongst their respective months as well as our most frequent listening time.

The data was cleaned with some assumptions made with regards to skipped songs, which will have an impact on the results - most likely changing the scale as opposed to the trend.

We also recognised that my listening times, both daily and monthly, were influenced by my activties and major upcoming events, unsurprisingly.
